# Core Writing Rules from *Science Research Writing*

## 1. Foundational Principles

### 1.1 Write for the Reader

| Principle | Meaning | Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Make function explicit | Let each sentence show not only information but also its role in the argument | Listing facts without showing why they matter |
| Wrap data in narrative | Guide interpretation with commentary | Presenting bare numbers |
| Assume less shared knowledge | Add enough context for a broad research reader | Opening with highly specific detail |
| Link sentences explicitly | Make the relation between neighboring sentences visible | Leaving transitions to the reader |

### 1.2 Reverse-Engineer Strong Papers

1. Select three to five strong recent papers from the target journal.
2. Analyze what each sentence does rather than what it says.
3. Infer a section model from those sentence functions.
4. Collect the recurring wording used to realize each function.
5. Adapt the model to your own manuscript.
6. Refresh the model as the field evolves.

### 1.3 Grammar Choices Carry Meaning

| Feature | Communicative meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Tense | Shows how permanent or generalizable the claim is |
| Voice | Assigns ownership of the action or finding |
| Article choice | Shows what knowledge is shared with the reader |

### 1.4 Paragraph Design

| Element | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| Single function | Give each paragraph one main communicative job |
| Oriented opening | Use the first sentence to guide the reader into the paragraph |
| Reasonable length | Keep paragraphs readable; avoid both blocks and fragments |
| Skim logic | Let paragraph openings form a logical map of the paper |

### 1.5 Four Ways to Connect Sentences

- Repeat a key term from the previous sentence.
- Use a stable noun phrase such as `this method` or `these results`.
- Use a semicolon only when two clauses are closely linked.
- Use a signal word only when the logic truly matches it.

## 2. Section Models

### 2.1 Introduction

Use four broad moves:

1. Establish topic importance and background.
2. Map existing work.
3. Identify the gap, conflict, or opportunity.
4. Present the current study.

Common ways to organize prior research:

- from general to specific,
- by method or theory,
- chronologically when the field developed through major external events.

Useful relation labels:

- `also observed`,
- `were the first to`,
- `a similar approach was used by`,
- `an alternative approach was proposed by`,
- `taken together, these studies suggest`,
- `these findings challenge`.

### 2.2 Methods

Menu of common moves:

1. Give an overview of the design, materials, or source.
2. Describe the procedure in enough detail.
3. Explain figures or setups when needed.
4. Cite previous methods and describe modifications.
5. Provide background facts that help the reader understand the method.
6. Note problems or limitations in the procedure.

Relation to prior methods:

- Same method: `according to`, `as described by`, `following`.
- Modified method: `adapted from`, `a modified version of`, `similar to`.
- Different method: `instead of`, `by contrast`, `with the following modifications`.

### 2.3 Results

Use four broad moves:

1. Reconnect the reader to the aim, hypothesis, or relevant method.
2. Present the key findings and guide interpretation.
3. State anomalies, limits, or unexpected patterns.
4. Indicate possible meaning without overinterpreting.

When presenting results, avoid bare numbers. Use calibrated commentary such as:

- `only 38%`,
- `as high as 38%`,
- `marked`,
- `substantial`,
- `approximately`,
- `rarely`.

### 2.4 Discussion

Think of the Discussion as a mirror of the Introduction:

- recall the study aim or answer,
- explain how the study addresses the gap,
- position the work in the literature,
- end with significance, limitation, application, or future work.

Typical relation labels:

- `confirm`,
- `extend`,
- `challenge`,
- `complement`,
- `shed new light on`,
- `pave the way for`.

### 2.5 Conclusion

Possible components:

1. summary of the paper,
2. statement of achievement,
3. background reminder,
4. research need or objective,
5. approach,
6. key result,
7. meaning,
8. limitation,
9. application,
10. knowledge advance,
11. future work.

### 2.6 Abstract

Three content zones often appear:

- importance, problem, and paper focus,
- method and main result,
- contribution, implication, or application.

Abstract-specific rules:

- keep ownership clear,
- avoid citations unless absolutely necessary,
- keep method detail lean,
- guide interpretation of numbers,
- combine functions when space is tight.

## 3. Tense as a Meaning System

### 3.1 Past vs Present

| Tense | Typical meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Simple past | Bounded finding or action in this study |
| Simple present | Stable fact, accepted knowledge, or stronger general claim |

### 3.2 Past vs Present Perfect

| Tense | Typical meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Simple past | Happened in the past; current relevance may be weak |
| Present perfect | Past pattern remains relevant to the present situation |

### 3.3 Quick Section Guide

| Section role | Typical tense |
| --- | --- |
| Background fact | Simple present |
| Prior research | Simple past or present perfect |
| Current study statement in Introduction | Simple present |
| Method steps | Simple past |
| Standard procedure or equipment description | Simple present |
| Figure description in Results | Simple present |
| Result acquisition | Simple past |
| Discussion meaning claims | Calibrated to confidence |
| Conclusion summary of achievement | Simple present or present perfect |

## 4. Modal Precision

### 4.1 Ability

- `can`, `is able to`
- `could`, `was able to`

### 4.2 Possibility

- `may`, `might`, `could`
- `may have`, `might have`, `could have`

### 4.3 Expectation

- `should`, `is expected to`, `is likely to`

### 4.4 High Confidence

- `must` for strong inference,
- `cannot` for impossibility.

### 4.5 Advice and Necessity

- Use `should` for recommendations.
- Use `must`, `need to`, or `have to` for necessity.
- Remember that `must not` means prohibition, not lack of necessity.

## 5. Causality

### 5.1 Strength Matters

| Strength | Typical verbs |
| --- | --- |
| Direct cause | `cause`, `produce`, `be due to` |
| Partial cause | `contribute to`, `be a factor in` |
| Process link | `lead to`, `result in`, `give rise to` |
| Origin | `originate in`, `arise from` |
| Weak link | `be related to`, `be linked to`, `be associated with` |

### 5.2 Articles Change Scope

- `a cause of` implies one cause among others.
- `the cause of` implies a unique or dominant cause.

### 5.3 Prepositions Change Direction

- `X results from Y`: Y causes X.
- `X results in Y`: X causes Y.

## 6. Passive Voice and Ownership

Passive sentences can hide who acted. Clarify whether the action belongs to:

- the current authors,
- cited authors,
- standard procedure,
- the setup shown in a figure,
- a field-level convention.

Ways to clarify:

- switch to active voice,
- add `in this study`,
- cite the source,
- use explicit noun phrases such as `the experiment described here`.

## 7. Risk Calibration

Lower claim strength when needed by using:

- frequency modifiers such as `often` or `in some cases`,
- quantity framing such as `in virtually all cases`,
- modal verbs such as `may`,
- framing phrases such as `it appears that` or `the evidence suggests`.

## 8. Spatial and Process Language

Useful spatial relations:

- `above`, `below`,
- `adjacent`, `next to`,
- `inside`, `within`,
- `parallel to`, `perpendicular to`.

Useful process verbs:

- `place`, `position`, `mount`,
- `arrange`, `align`,
- `connect`, `embed`,
- `intersect`, `surround`.

## 9. Professional Ways to State Problems and Limits

Three common strategies:

- minimize the severity when justified: `minor`, `negligible`,
- minimize author blame when justified: `unavoidable`, `limited by`,
- preserve value despite the limit: `nevertheless`, `reasonably robust`.

For future work:

- `future work should`,
- `further work is needed`,
- `work is currently underway`.

## 10. Articles and Shared Knowledge

### 10.1 Common Mass Nouns

In scientific prose, words such as `evidence`, `research`, `information`, `equipment`, and `knowledge` are usually treated as mass nouns.

### 10.2 Communicative Logic

- `a` or `an` introduces something not yet shared.
- `the` marks something identifiable from context or shared knowledge.
- Zero article can broaden scope or audience framing.

## 11. Sequencing Language

Useful time markers by function:

- before: `prior to`, `beforehand`, `previously`,
- first step: `initially`, `at the beginning`,
- ordered sequence: `then`, `subsequently`, `following`,
- short delay: `soon`, `shortly after`,
- later stage: `eventually`, `later`,
- immediate succession: `as soon as`, `once`, `immediately`,
- final step: `finally`, `at the end`,
- after completion: `afterwards`, `subsequently`.

## 12. Signal Words

### 12.1 Cause

- `because`,
- `due to`,
- `since` and `as` only when they cannot be read temporally.

### 12.2 Result

- `therefore`,
- `consequently`,
- `as a result`,
- `thus`.

### 12.3 Contrast

- `however`,
- `whereas`,
- `by contrast`,
- `in contrast`.

### 12.4 Unexpected Contrast

- `although`,
- `despite`,
- `nevertheless`,
- `nonetheless`.

### 12.5 Addition

- `in addition`,
- `moreover`,
- `furthermore`,
- `also`.

### 12.6 Topic Shift

- `regarding`,
- `with respect to`,
- `turning now to`.

Do not start every sentence with a signal word; overuse makes the prose mechanical.

## 13. Citation Placement and Ownership

- Put a citation next to the claim it supports.
- Avoid stacks of end-of-sentence citations when each source supports a different idea.
- In Methods, cite the original method, then state modifications and their rationale.

## 14. Adverb Position

`Only` changes meaning depending on where it appears:

- `Only we analysed...`
- `We only analysed...`
- `We analysed only models...`

Check position carefully before finalizing the sentence.

## 15. Ambiguity in `-ing` Forms

An `-ing` phrase can imply:

- means,
- cause,
- timing,
- result,
- reduced relative clause,
- condition,
- explanation.

Expand it into a full clause when the intended relation is not unmistakable.

## 16. Lexical Consistency

- Keep one term for one concept.
- Do not chase synonyms to avoid repetition.
- Let repetition help the reader track the concept.

Examples of pairs that are not interchangeable:

- `alternately` vs `alternatively`,
- `effective` vs `efficient`,
- `imply` vs `infer`,
- `criterion` vs `criteria`.

## 17. Compound Nouns and Hyphens

Hyphens glue words into a single modifier. Compare:

- `five centimetre-wide layers`,
- `five-centimetre-wide layers`.

The visual grouping changes the meaning.

## 18. Titles

Evaluate titles for:

- average length in the target journal,
- acronym transparency,
- keyword coverage,
- grammatical clarity,
- common title patterns in the journal.

Prefer titles that tell the reader what the paper contributes, not just the broad topic.

## 19. Prepositions

Prepositions often shift meaning:

- `substituted for` vs `substituted with`,
- `evidence of` vs `evidence for`,
- `coated with` vs `coated by`.

`With` is especially risky because it can imply cause, possession, timing, or manner. Replace it with a more precise phrase when needed.

## 20. Prefix Awareness

Common scientific prefixes help decode terminology:

- `anti-`, `auto-`, `hetero-`, `homo-`,
- `hyper-`, `hypo-`,
- `inter-`, `intra-`,
- `macro-`, `micro-`, `nano-`,
- `pre-`, `post-`, `trans-`, `sub-`, `super-`.

## 21. Final Checklist

Ask these questions before finalizing:

- Is the paper organized around reader needs rather than author memory?
- Does each paragraph have one clear communicative function?
- Does each sentence connect explicitly to the previous one?
- Is every tense choice meaningful?
- Is passive voice still clear about ownership?
- Are articles, pronouns, and prepositions precise?
- Are claims calibrated to evidence strength?
- Are citations attached to the claims they support?
- Is terminology stable throughout?

## 22. Core Maxims

- The goal is not merely to make understanding possible, but to make misunderstanding difficult.
- Data alone do not tell the reader what matters.
- Knowing what to say is not the same as knowing how to write it.
- Familiarity with your own draft makes ambiguity harder to see.
- Good organization can outweigh small language errors; flawless grammar cannot rescue weak structure.
- Show the reader the wall before you discuss the bricks.
- The space between sentences is dangerous.
- The thesaurus is not your friend in scientific prose.

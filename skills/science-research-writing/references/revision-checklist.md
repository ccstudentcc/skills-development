# Revision Checklist

Use this file as the final quality gate after drafting or revising. It is also the fastest way to diagnose a weak passage when the user asks `what is wrong with this paragraph`.

## Preflight

Confirm these before you rewrite:

- What is the task: `draft`, `revise`, `diagnose`, `compress`, or `expand`?
- What section is this text supposed to belong to?
- What source material is authoritative: notes, figures, rough prose, or finished text?
- What may not be changed: claim strength, numbers, citations, terminology, or voice?

If any of those are unclear, state the assumption instead of silently inventing one.

## Pass 1: Structure

Check section and paragraph logic first.

- Does the section perform the rhetorical job expected for its location in the manuscript?
- Does each paragraph perform one main communicative function?
- Does each paragraph opening orient the reader?
- Is the gap, method choice, result meaning, limitation, or implication stated where the reader expects it?
- Can the reader skim paragraph openings and recover the logic of the passage?

## Pass 2: Evidence Discipline

Check whether the prose stays faithful to the available evidence.

- Are data wrapped in interpretation rather than dumped as bare numbers?
- Are causal verbs, novelty claims, and certainty levels supported by the evidence?
- Are limitations or anomalies stated when they materially affect interpretation?
- Are comparisons with prior work labeled clearly as confirmation, extension, contradiction, qualification, or complement?
- Does the passage avoid importing mechanisms, motivations, or literature claims that were never supplied?

## Pass 3: Sentence Control

Check local clarity sentence by sentence.

- Does each sentence connect clearly to the previous sentence?
- Is tense chosen deliberately rather than mechanically?
- Is ownership clear whenever passive voice appears?
- Are articles, pronouns, and noun phrases precise enough for the intended reader?
- Does any sentence force the reader to infer why the information matters?

## Pass 4: Terminology and Consistency

- Is the same concept named the same way throughout the passage?
- Are acronyms introduced before reuse?
- Have vague synonyms or shorthand labels been removed where they blur meaning?
- Are key comparative terms such as `increase`, `improvement`, `difference`, or `association` used consistently?
- If tables or figures are involved, do titles, legends, and prose repeat the same key terms rather than drifting into new labels?

## Pass 5: Delivery Check

Before returning the text:

- Verify that numbers, comparisons, and citations still match the source.
- Verify that abstract numbers, body-text numbers, and table or figure values do not contradict one another.
- Verify that each citation is placed next to the claim it supports and does not point to a source that fails to support that claim.
- Verify that the revision did not strengthen the claim beyond the evidence.
- Verify that the prose now answers the reader's likely `So what?` question.
- Verify that the final wording is still section-appropriate rather than globally polished but rhetorically misplaced.
- Verify that any reviewer-facing text is specific about what changed and where.

## Diagnosis Output Pattern

When the user asks for diagnosis rather than a full rewrite, structure the response in this order:

1. `Main problem` - name the dominant issue in one sentence.
2. `Why it hurts the reader` - explain the effect on clarity, logic, or credibility.
3. `Where it shows up` - point to the exact sentence or local pattern.
4. `How to repair it` - give a concrete rewrite direction.

## Stop Conditions

The passage is ready to return when all of the following are true:

- the structure matches the section's job,
- the evidence boundary is respected,
- sentence ownership and logic are clear,
- terminology is stable,
- no major ambiguity remains about what the study found, means, or does not support.

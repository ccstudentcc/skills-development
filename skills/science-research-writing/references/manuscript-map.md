# Manuscript Map

## Table of Contents

- Introduction
- Methods
- Results
- Discussion
- Conclusion
- Abstract
- Title

## Introduction

Purpose: establish importance, map prior work, identify the unresolved problem, and introduce the present study.

Use these moves:
- Open with field relevance or stable background facts.
- Summarize the existing research landscape instead of listing papers without purpose.
- Expose the gap, limitation, conflict, or unmet need.
- Present the present study with objective, approach, and contribution.

Watch for:
- Starting too narrowly with technical detail.
- Citing studies one by one without synthesis.
- Announcing the study before the gap is visible.

## Methods

Purpose: let the reader understand what was done, why those choices were made, and what constraints shaped the procedure.

Use these moves:
- Open with the overall design, materials, cohort, or experimental setup.
- Describe the procedure in a reproducible sequence.
- Name borrowed methods and explain modifications when relevant.
- Add brief rationale only when it helps the reader interpret the choice.
- State known limitations or procedural constraints when they matter downstream.

Watch for:
- Turning the section into raw chronology without structure.
- Mixing background theory with procedural detail.
- Using passive sentences that hide whether the action belongs to this study or prior work.

## Results

Purpose: present the findings, guide the reader through the evidence, and indicate what the data show without drifting into full discussion.

Use these moves:
- Start with the overall result or the figure-level point.
- Present the key numbers, comparisons, or patterns.
- Compare with expectations, models, or prior findings only when that helps interpretation.
- Acknowledge anomalies, uncertainty, or technical limits when needed.
- Use evaluative language carefully so numbers are not left uninterpreted.

Watch for:
- Listing results with no guidance about what matters.
- Repeating all figure details in prose.
- Moving too early into speculative explanation.

## Discussion

Purpose: interpret the findings, position them in the literature, state contribution, and define the limits of the claim.

Use these moves:
- Re-open with the study's key achievement or answer.
- Explain what the main findings mean.
- Compare the findings with prior work using explicit relation labels such as confirm, extend, challenge, or complement.
- State limitations and explain how they affect interpretation.
- Close with significance, application, or future work.

Watch for:
- Restating the Results section without interpretation.
- Making claims stronger than the evidence allows.
- Mentioning limitations only as a ritual after making an unqualified claim.

## Conclusion

Purpose: give the reader a compact final account of what the study achieved and why it matters.

Use these moves:
- Summarize the research problem or objective briefly.
- Restate the core result or contribution.
- State the meaning, application, or implication.
- Mention major limitation or future work only if it sharpens the takeaway.

Watch for:
- Repeating the abstract mechanically.
- Adding brand-new evidence.
- Ending with generic claims that could fit any paper.

## Abstract

Purpose: help the reader decide whether to read the paper by presenting the problem, approach, main finding, and significance in compressed form.

Use these moves:
- Introduce the topic and why it matters.
- State the problem, gap, or need.
- State the approach or method briefly.
- State the main result with guided interpretation.
- State the contribution, implication, or application.

Watch for:
- Bare numbers without commentary.
- Too much method detail.
- Ambiguity about whether statements describe prior work or the present study.

## Title

Purpose: tell the reader what the paper contributes, not just what topic it touches.

Prefer titles that:
- name the substantive topic,
- reveal the contribution, method, or angle,
- avoid unexplained acronyms unless the target audience certainly knows them.

Watch for:
- working titles that describe only the field,
- dense noun stacks,
- novelty claims that the paper does not support.

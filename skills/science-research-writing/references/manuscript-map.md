# Manuscript Map

Use this file when the task is mainly about section structure, paragraph moves, or deciding what belongs in a given part of a manuscript.

## Quick Navigation

- Start with `Routing Guide` when the user has not named the section yet.
- Jump to `Introduction`, `Methods`, `Results`, or `Discussion` for core IMRAD drafting and diagnosis.
- Jump to `Conclusion`, `Abstract`, or `Title` for compression-heavy polishing work.

Before writing, identify:

1. The section and subtask: `draft`, `revise`, `diagnose`, `compress`, or `expand`.
2. The source material you are allowed to use: notes, bullet points, figures, rough prose, or finished text.
3. The evidence boundary: do not invent mechanisms, comparisons, or claims that are not supported by the supplied material.
4. The reader need for that section: what question should the reader stop asking after the paragraph or section is read?

## Introduction

### Reader Goal

Let the reader understand why the problem matters, what is already known, what remains unresolved, and why this study is the next relevant step.

### Build Order

1. Start with field relevance or stable background.
2. Map prior work by function, not by citation order.
3. Surface the unresolved gap, conflict, limitation, or unmet need.
4. Introduce the present study with objective, approach, and contribution.

### Sentence Jobs

Use some or all of these jobs in order:

- establish importance,
- define the narrow topic,
- synthesize what prior work has already shown,
- expose the missing piece,
- state the study aim,
- preview the study approach or contribution.

### Drafting Pattern

Use a paragraph skeleton like:

`Field relevance -> current knowledge -> unresolved problem -> present study`

Useful rewrite prompts for yourself:

- `Replace citation-by-citation listing with grouped synthesis by method, claim, or limitation.`
- `Make the gap visible before the study sentence appears.`
- `Turn general background into a runway for the exact problem this paper addresses.`

### Failure Modes

- Starts too narrowly with technical detail the broad reader has not earned yet.
- Uses citations as a list instead of as support for a map of the literature.
- Introduces the current study before the gap has become necessary.
- Uses novelty language without establishing what is genuinely missing.

## Methods

### Reader Goal

Let the reader see what was done, why those choices were made, what prior methods were reused, and which constraints matter for interpreting the results.

### Build Order

1. Open with the design, materials, cohort, or setup.
2. Present the procedure in a reproducible sequence.
3. Name borrowed methods and state whether they were followed, adapted, or replaced.
4. Add rationale only where it changes interpretation.
5. Note procedural limitations when they affect downstream claims.

### Sentence Jobs

- name the design or source,
- locate materials, participants, or equipment,
- describe the key procedural sequence,
- justify important choices,
- cite inherited methods,
- state modifications,
- disclose constraints or limitations.

### Drafting Pattern

Use a paragraph skeleton like:

`Overall design -> key steps -> source or modification -> rationale or constraint`

When the source is notes rather than prose, first extract:

- what was done,
- in what order,
- with what material or instrument,
- for what purpose,
- with which cited precedent.

### Failure Modes

- Reads like a lab notebook dump instead of a structured procedure.
- Mixes background theory and procedural steps in the same sentence block.
- Hides ownership so the reader cannot tell which step belongs to this study and which comes from prior work.
- Explains every choice equally, leaving the important decisions buried.

## Results

### Reader Goal

Help the reader see what the evidence shows, which patterns matter, and what interpretation is safe at this stage.

### Build Order

1. Re-anchor the reader to the aim, comparison, or figure-level question.
2. State the main pattern or finding first.
3. Report the key numbers or contrasts.
4. Mention anomalies, limits, or exceptions when they matter.
5. Add minimal interpretation that helps the reader understand significance without turning the section into a Discussion.

### Sentence Jobs

- orient to the comparison,
- state the headline finding,
- report the supporting values,
- compare groups, time points, or conditions,
- note exceptions,
- state restrained meaning.

### Drafting Pattern

Use a paragraph skeleton like:

`Question or figure focus -> headline result -> key values -> exception or boundary -> restrained significance`

For figure-driven prose, answer these questions in order:

1. What should the reader notice first?
2. Which values or contrasts make that point credible?
3. What limit, anomaly, or caveat keeps the claim honest?

### Failure Modes

- Lists data without telling the reader why the pattern matters.
- Repeats every plotted detail instead of selecting the result-level point.
- Drifts into mechanism speculation better reserved for the Discussion.
- Uses evaluation words such as `marked`, `substantial`, or `only` without supporting context.

## Discussion

### Reader Goal

Interpret the findings, position the study in relation to prior work, define the contribution, and state the limits of what can be concluded.

### Build Order

1. Re-open with the study's main answer or achievement.
2. Interpret what the findings mean.
3. Compare the findings with prior work using explicit relation labels.
4. State limitations and say how they constrain interpretation.
5. Close with significance, application, or future work.

### Sentence Jobs

- restate the answer,
- interpret the result,
- compare with prior literature,
- explain agreement or disagreement,
- define the limitation,
- state implication, application, or next step.

### Relation Labels

Prefer explicit relation labels such as:

- `confirm`,
- `extend`,
- `challenge`,
- `complement`,
- `qualify`,
- `leave unresolved`.

### Failure Modes

- Restates the Results with little added meaning.
- Makes stronger causal or general claims than the evidence supports.
- Mentions limitations only after already presenting the claim as unqualified truth.
- Compares with prior work vaguely instead of naming the relation between studies.

## Conclusion

### Reader Goal

Leave the reader with a compact, accurate account of what the study achieved and why it matters.

### Build Order

1. Briefly restate the problem or objective.
2. Restate the core result or contribution.
3. State the significance, implication, or application.
4. Mention limitation or future work only if it sharpens the takeaway.

### Sentence Jobs

- restate the problem,
- name the contribution,
- state the meaning,
- state the practical or conceptual takeaway,
- define the remaining boundary.

### Failure Modes

- Repeats the abstract mechanically.
- Adds new evidence or analysis.
- Ends with generic impact language that could fit almost any paper.

## Abstract

### Reader Goal

Give a compressed but self-sufficient account of the problem, approach, main finding, and importance so the reader can decide whether to read further.

### Build Order

1. State the topic and why it matters.
2. State the problem, gap, or need.
3. State the approach briefly.
4. State the main result with interpretation.
5. State the contribution, implication, or application.

### Sentence Jobs

- set context,
- state the unresolved need,
- name the approach,
- state the headline finding,
- state why the finding matters.

### Compression Rules

- Keep method detail lean unless it is the main novelty.
- Keep ownership clear: the reader must know what belongs to this study.
- Do not leave numbers uninterpreted.
- Combine functions when the abstract length requires it, but do not drop the logic chain.

### Failure Modes

- Reads like a list of disconnected facts.
- Uses bare values with no explanation of direction or importance.
- Spends too many words on routine method detail.
- Blurs prior knowledge and present-study findings.

## Title

### Reader Goal

Tell the reader what the paper contributes, not only the broad topic area.

### Build Order

1. Identify the substantive topic.
2. Identify the study angle, method, population, or contribution.
3. Keep only the terms that materially help the right reader recognize the paper.

### Title Checks

- Does the title reveal the paper's real object, method, or claim?
- Is any acronym safe for the target audience?
- Is the title carrying too many nouns in a row?
- Does any novelty claim need proof that the paper does not provide?

### Failure Modes

- Sounds like a placeholder working title.
- States the field but not the paper's contribution.
- Uses dense noun stacks or unexplained abbreviations.
- Uses `novel`, `first`, or `effective` without support.
